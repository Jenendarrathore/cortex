# The Voyager Program

## Overview

The Voyager program is a NASA mission consisting of two robotic interstellar
probes, Voyager 1 and Voyager 2. Voyager 2 launched first, on August 20, 1977,
followed by Voyager 1 on September 5, 1977. Despite launching later, Voyager 1
took a faster trajectory and overtook its twin. Both were built and operated by
the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Pasadena, California.

## Power Source

Each spacecraft is powered by three radioisotope thermoelectric generators
(RTGs) fueled by plutonium-238. At launch the RTGs produced about 470 watts of
electrical power. Because the plutonium decays, output drops roughly 4 watts per
year, which is why instruments are switched off over time to conserve energy.

## Instruments

Each probe carried eleven scientific instruments, including an imaging science
system, an infrared spectrometer, a magnetometer, and a plasma wave subsystem.
The cameras on both spacecraft were powered down in the early 1990s to save
energy after the planetary flybys were complete.

## The Grand Tour

Voyager 2 remains the only spacecraft to have visited all four giant planets:
Jupiter (1979), Saturn (1981), Uranus (1986), and Neptune (1989). This route
exploited a rare alignment of the outer planets that occurs about once every
176 years. Voyager 1 visited only Jupiter and Saturn before being redirected
toward interstellar space to study Saturn's moon Titan.

## The Golden Record

Each Voyager carries a gold-plated copper phonograph record called the Golden
Record. It contains 115 images, greetings in 55 languages, and a 90-minute
selection of music from many cultures. The record was intended as a message to
any intelligent extraterrestrial life that might find it. The committee that
chose its contents was chaired by astronomer Carl Sagan.

## Interstellar Space

Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause and entered interstellar space on August 25,
2012, becoming the first human-made object to do so. Voyager 2 followed on
November 5, 2018. Signals from the probes, traveling at the speed of light,
take more than 20 hours to reach Earth and are received by NASA's Deep Space
Network.
